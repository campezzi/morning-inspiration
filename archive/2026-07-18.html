1904 Olympic Marathon
Thomas Hicks — 1904

James Edward Sullivan, the meet's chief organizer, put a single water source on the 24-mile course — a well at the halfway mark — because he wanted to study what he called "purposeful dehydration." Fred Lorz crossed the line first, had a wreath set on his head by Alice Roosevelt, and was about to receive the gold medal when spectators mentioned he'd covered eleven of the miles by car. Thomas Hicks, who had in fact run the whole thing, made it to the stadium on repeated doses of strychnine and brandy administered by his own handlers, hallucinating so badly by the end that they carried him across the line. He was declared the winner, then treated by four doctors who kept him from dying in front of the crowd that had just watched him win.
