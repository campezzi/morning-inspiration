The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge
Rainer Maria Rilke — 1910
So, people do come here in order to live; I would sooner have thought one died here. I have been out. I saw: hospitals.
— Rainer Maria Rilke, The Notebooks of Malte Laurids Brigge (1910)

Rilke arrived in Paris in 1902 and began writing in a notebook that would take seven years to finish. Malte Laurids Brigge is his stand-in — a young Danish poet living on very little, in a city that does nothing to keep its suffering private. The notebooks accumulate: the man who collapses in the street, the pregnant woman pressing her hand against a wall to make sure it is still there, the hospitals. There is no plot, only a consciousness being worn down by proximity. He wrote the first sentence and somehow never stopped.
