The Raft of the Medusa
Théodore Géricault — 1819

Géricault built this on a canvas nearly five meters wide, filling it with bodies — the dying, the dead, the still-hopeful craning toward a ship barely visible at the edge of the horizon. He visited morgues to study corpses, borrowed severed limbs from hospitals, had a scale model of the raft built in his studio. The painting caused a scandal at the 1819 Salon: it was read as a political attack on the restored monarchy that had suppressed the story of the wreck and bungled the rescue. Géricault died at 32, two years after it was finished. The raft stayed.
