The Gothic Arch
Giovanni Battista Piranesi — 1761

Piranesi called the series Le Carceri d'Invenzione — Imaginary Prisons — and never explained what any of it is for. A gothic arch swallows the frame; stairways climb toward landings that open onto more stairways, a bridge crosses a gap to nowhere in particular, and a chain hangs from a pulley with no visible job. He etched the first version in 1750, in his twenties, then came back eleven years later and darkened it — thicker shadows, more smoke, more machinery crowded into the same impossible room. No prisoners appear anywhere in the sixteen plates. The space just keeps going.
